A vital aspect in our daily lives is that we are able to successfully learn the relationships between our actions and their outcomes. These can be rather simple, mechanistic action-outcome relationships, such as the notion that turning a switch will result in the light being turned on or that stepping on a wasp will result in a painful sting. But they can also be highly complex relationships, such as the learning that planting crop will only result in a good harvest on certain pastures or what actions one needs to take when ill. Learning these relationships is paramount to successful navigation of our social world and our continued existence speaks to our overall capabilities in learning such relationships. Nonetheless, humans are prone to errors in judgement. False beliefs such as superstitions or the belief in the success of alternative medical treatments are pervasive – even in situations in which people encounter firsthand evidence and when they should be highly motivated to learn the true underlying relationship.  
In my PhD project, I investigate human contingency learning from a social cognition perspective. My focus is on when and why humans err in their judgements and how such erroneous beliefs are updated during information sampling as people test the validity of their beliefs. My research not only adds to the understanding of social functioning but is highly important for developing interventions in the context of health, work, and well-being.
